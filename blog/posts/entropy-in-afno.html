What an AFNO actually learns: an entropy-eye view

I spent a few weeks poking at NVIDIA’s FourCastNet by feeding it inputs it was never trained on — pure white noise on one hand, Kolmogorov-distributed turbulence on the other — and asking a single question: how does the spectral content evolve as data passes through the AFNO blocks?

The setup

For each layer $\ell$ and each input ensemble, I compute the radially-averaged energy spectrum $E_k(\ell)$ from the activations and define a normalized spectral distribution

$$ \hat{p}_k(\ell) = \frac{|E_k(\ell)|^2}{\sum_{k'} |E_{k'}(\ell)|^2}. $$

The spectral entropy is then the standard Shannon entropy of this distribution,

$$ S(\ell) = -\sum_{k} \hat{p}_k(\ell) \log \hat{p}_k(\ell). $$

What happens

For white noise, $S(\ell)$ stays roughly flat across depth — the network has no reason to redistribute energy in $k$-space, and it doesn’t. For Kolmogorov inputs (energy spectrum $\sim k^{-5/3}$), $S(\ell)$ collapses over the first few blocks and then plateaus. The shape of the collapse is reproducible across seeds.

That’s the cute result: the network has effectively learned a soft band-pass filter in the inertial range, and you can read it off the entropy curve without ever looking at the weights.

Why I think this matters

Neural operators are sold as universal function approximators in $L^2$, but in practice the training distribution does most of the work. Treating $S(\ell)$ as a diagnostic gives you a cheap, model-agnostic way to ask where in the architecture the physics gets baked in.
